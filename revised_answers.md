Several surprises emerged.

First, the IV2SLS specification never kept under the v1 evaluator. It failed `spec_stability` because the OLS multiverse is the wrong reference for a LATE on compliers. The agent could have detected this and proposed a separate IV multiverse, but `program.md` does not let it modify `prepare.py`. This is the cost of a fixed evaluation rule: the rule that protects against gaming also forecloses a legitimate research direction. The v2 design (see §4.3 and `CHANGES.md`) addresses this with a parallel IV multiverse; under v2, iter01 scores 4/5.

Second, the simplification attempt at iter05 (drop `married`) revealed an unexpected role for that variable: it was driving the heteroskedasticity that the HC3 standard errors were correcting for. Once `married` was removed, the Breusch–Pagan p-value rose from 0.0055 to 0.37 — the residuals became homoskedastic. The agent did not flag this in its commit message, but it is visible in the `results.tsv` row.

Third, the parsimony tiebreaker at iter11 worked as intended. The added covariate produced a CV-MSE improvement of 0.005% — well within the 0.5% keep threshold, so the standard keep rule would have accepted it. The tiebreaker refused to pay an extra parameter for essentially zero gain. Without this rule the model would slowly accumulate epsilon-improving covariates.

Fourth, the hard identification rule at iter10 blocked the failure mode the workflow was designed to prevent. Trimming 5% of outliers produced the lowest CV-MSE in the entire run, by a wide margin (0.0930), but `educ` drifted to 0.0510 — just below the multiverse 5% bound. Without the hard rule, this would have been kept and reported as a credible point estimate. With the hard rule, the workflow self-arrested at the credibility boundary.

The agent's final kept specification is identical to the baseline except for the 2.5% `lwage` trim. That this is the conclusion of an autonomous search over thirteen specifications — including IV2SLS, ability proxies, region dummies, parental-structure controls, and three different trim levels — is itself the result. The data does not support a more complex specification than the trimmed-sample Mincer baseline.

## 4.1 What Worked Well  *(polished)*

Three parts of the workflow worked better than I expected, and the value of each only became visible during execution.

The first was the composite multi-dimensional `spec_score` rather than a single fit metric. When I designed it, five binary dimensions felt arbitrary; in practice, the five dimensions caught five different failure modes during the run. Iter02 failed on diagnostics (VIF = 17.2 from region-dummy collinearity). Iter03 failed on diagnostics again but for a different reason (VIF = 69 from IQ–KWW correlation), even though it produced the textbook Griliches ability-bias correction (`educ` dropping from 0.072 to 0.058). Iter05 failed on out-of-sample fit, having passed everything else. Iter10 failed on `spec_stability` while producing the lowest CV-MSE of any iteration in the run. A single-metric reward would have treated those five failures identically; the multi-dimensional reward distinguished them and told the agent, in each case, *which dimension* it had broken. That information became the basis for the next iteration's direction, and it made the journal in `results.tsv` interpretable after the fact.

The second was the operationalisation of the three human checkpoints. I had written them as instructions in `program.md`, but it was not clear whether the agent would actually pause at the right moments rather than describing the pause and continuing. In practice it stopped cleanly at all three: at Checkpoint 1 after the baseline confirmation, at Checkpoint 2 when the IV2SLS swap fired the estimator-class trigger, and at Checkpoint 3 after five consecutive discards. The Checkpoint 2 audit produced an explicit identification statement, a first-stage F comparison to Stock–Yogo, and a literature comparison to Card 1999 and Heckman–Lochner–Todd 2006 — the audit a referee would ask for. The Checkpoint 3 pause was even more useful. The five-discard plateau was real, and the redirection toward sample restrictions (rather than further control-set tweaks) produced both of the run's KEEP decisions back-to-back. Without the checkpoint, the agent would have continued tweaking controls and would not have found the trim move that broke the plateau.

The third was the *defensive* design of the reward function. Two rules I added on the assumption they would rarely fire actually fired and rescued the run. The hard identification rule — discard regardless of fit if identification fails — caught iter10. That iteration produced a CV-MSE of 0.0930, the lowest of any specification by a wide margin (the next-best was 0.107). Without the hard rule the score would have been 4/5 and the agent would have kept it; with the rule it scored 3/5 and was discarded. The `educ` coefficient at that specification (0.0510) sat just below the multiverse 5th percentile (0.0508), which is the credibility boundary the multiverse defines. The hard rule prevented the agent from quietly walking the slope down by over-trimming. The parsimony tiebreaker did similar work at iter11, where adding a covariate improved CV-MSE by 0.005% — well within the 0.5% keep threshold — but the tiebreaker refused to spend an extra parameter for a non-improvement. Neither rule was conceptually difficult to write. The lesson is that those small "always" rules, the ones that look redundant during design, do most of the work that distinguishes a search journal from a specification-mining log.

Two design choices in `program.md` contributed visibly to smoother execution. Centering experience before squaring it pushed the baseline VIF down to 5.7, which made VIF a non-issue for every baseline-adjacent specification and let diagnostics discriminate among the alternatives. Embedding a JSON block at the bottom of every `analysis.py` output gave the agent a typed parse target alongside the human-readable grep block; across thirteen iterations there was no parsing failure. Both are small; both saved an iteration's worth of debugging.

One surprise I would put under "the agent did something creative." Between iter06 and iter09, after Checkpoint 3's redirection, the agent did not just try the proposed 1% trim and stop. It treated outlier trimming as a one-dimensional parameter and walked it: 1% (KEEP), then 2.5% (KEEP, new best), then 5% (DISCARD on the hard rule). The 5% step probed the credibility ceiling — it was a deliberate test of where the multiverse cut off, not an accident. I had not specified that kind of sweep in `program.md`; the agent generated it from the structure of the problem after Checkpoint 3 had pointed it at sample restrictions.

## 4.2 What Went Wrong  *(polished)*

The most informative failure was not a crash but a structural one: the workflow could not reward the most economically interesting move it considered.

Iter01 — IV2SLS with `nearc4` — is the canonical Card 1995 specification. The first-stage F was 15.75, above the Stock–Yogo threshold; the LATE on compliers was 0.1249, inside the Card 1999 / Heckman-Lochner-Todd 2006 literature range; the exclusion restriction has a defensible verbal statement. By any external standard, this is the most defensible specification the agent produced. By the workflow's internal standard, it scored 3 out of 5 and was discarded. The reason is structural: the multiverse encoded in `prepare.py` is OLS-only, so the `spec_stability` dimension always fires against an IV estimand that is, by economic theory, expected to *exceed* the OLS distribution. The `oos_fit` dimension worked similarly against IV: 2SLS trades efficiency for consistency, so its training residual variance is always going to be worse than the OLS CV-MSE.

I described this in the Checkpoint 2 audit as "the multiverse design choice biting us," but on reflection it goes deeper. The reward function as written rewards specifications that are *stable inside the OLS frame*. It has no category for "credibly identified estimand from a different population." If I had foreseen this, I would have built two parallel multiverses into `prepare.py` upfront (one OLS, one IV), or added a separate identification-strategy dimension that scored IV first-stage strength independently and was not penalised by multiverse mismatch. Iter12 confirmed the structural nature of the problem: running the same IV strategy on the 2.5%-trimmed sample brought the LATE down to 0.115 (slightly closer to the OLS multiverse) but still discarded for the same reasons. The IV path could not score, regardless of which sub-move the agent tried. **The v2 fix in `CHANGES.md` implements the dual-multiverse redesign; under v2, iter01 scores 4/5.**

The second failure was narrower but related. The agent's commit messages were honest about what each change was, but they did not always articulate *why* the agent thought the change might address the previous iteration's weakest dimension. Most iterations passed identification, parsimony, and stability, and failed only on diagnostics or `oos_fit`. The agent's stated rationale tended to be econometrically motivated (Griliches ability bias, regional wage differentials, Mincer human-capital theory) rather than score-motivated. The two motivations usually coincided, but when they did not, the agent followed the econometric framing and ran into diagnostic walls it could have anticipated. Iter03 is the clearest case: any educated guess about IQ–KWW collinearity should have predicted the VIF problem before the run, but the commit message framed the move purely as "Griliches–Heckman ability bias" with no mention of multicollinearity risk. A better `program.md` would have asked, in the per-iteration template, "which dimension does this change target, and what is the risk to other dimensions?" — a small change that would have made the search more strategic and the log more diagnostic.

There were no crashes during the autonomous run. The crash at iter13 (`KeyError: ['nearc3']`) was deliberately injected after the loop ended, to demonstrate the crash-handling path. That itself is informative: in thirteen iterations operating on a single 3010-row CSV with a fixed set of columns, the agent never wrote broken Python. The narrow editable surface (one marked region of one file) and the static input data jointly made crashes unlikely; the `program.md` crash rule was load-bearing only because the workflow had to demonstrate it existed.

The single moment that came closest to a wrong call was iter05 — dropping `married`. The result looked like a candidate for a parsimony keep: VIF fell to 5.1, Breusch–Pagan stopped rejecting (p = 0.37, the first non-rejecting BP in the run), and the model genuinely got simpler. The agent discarded it because CV-MSE rose 3.4%, too much for a parsimony keep. The call was correct under the rules, but it surfaced an issue with the rules themselves: the workflow has no machinery for noticing *why* a diagnostic changed. The shift in BP from 0.0055 (reject) to 0.37 (don't reject) when `married` was removed is a real finding — it tells you that `married` was the variable driving the heteroskedasticity HC3 was correcting for. That finding never made it into `results.tsv` because the row records only what BP did, not what it implied. A more linguistically-aware workflow would have noticed.

## 4.3 Workflow Improvements  *(polished)*

If I redesigned the workflow now, I would change four things.

First, I would build two parallel multiverses into `prepare.py` — one OLS, one IV — and have the `spec_stability` dimension select the right reference set based on the estimator the iteration uses. The single-multiverse design was the workflow's most expensive simplification. It made the OLS branch comparable across iterations but made the IV branch incomparable to anything, so the IV path could never score even when it was the most defensible specification the agent tried. With dual multiverses, the IV LATE at 0.1249 would have been judged against the IV multiverse rather than the OLS one, and the workflow could have produced two parallel research products: a best OLS specification and a best IV specification, with separate stability bands. **This redesign is now implemented in v2** — see `prepare.py::iv_specification_curve` and the smoke test at `scripts/smoke_iv_v2.py`. Under v2, iter01 scores 4/5 (up from 3/5), with `spec_stability` passing against the IV multiverse (median 0.143, 5/95 band 0.091–0.575).

Second, I would add a per-iteration template that asks the agent to write two things before the change: (i) which dimension the change is targeting, (ii) which dimension it is most at risk of breaking. This is one paragraph of additional prompting in `program.md`. It would have caught iter03 (IQ+KWW collinearity was foreseeable) and iter04 (same problem, IQ-only); it would have turned the commit message from a description of the move into a small piece of pre-registration. The text-based search log gets better the more pre-registered each step is.

Third, I would split the diagnostic dimension. The current single binary fires identically for "BP rejects and you used HC3" (correctly handled), "VIF over 15" (collinearity), and "RESET rejects" (functional-form misspecification). Three different problems with three different remedies, but only one bit of information in the reward. Splitting the dimension would give the agent more directed feedback at the cost of a slightly more complex score (the composite could still be reported as 0–5 by collapsing them on output).

Fourth, I would experiment with a two-agent architecture: one agent that proposes the change and writes the commit message (the "researcher"), and a second agent that reads only `prepare.py` and `results.tsv` and challenges the proposal before it is committed (the "referee"). The referee would not know which dimension last failed; its job would be to predict, for the proposed change, what each dimension is likely to do and to flag the move only if its predictions agree with the design intent. This is computationally cheap (one extra forward pass per iteration), and it imitates the social structure of pre-publication peer review. I would not expect it to change the keep/discard pattern much, but I would expect it to make the journal substantially more legible: each row would have a forecast attached to it, and disagreements between forecast and outcome would themselves be a source of insight. Implementations to draw on already exist — CoPaper.AI's PaperAgent (supervisor + four child agents with skill-based routing) and Agent Laboratory's Phase-2 split (Plan / Code / Execute agents) both encode the pattern.

There are two changes I would *not* make. I would not loosen the hard identification rule, even though it cost the workflow the v1 IV result. The rule did the work hard rules are for — it prevented the workflow from being tricked into reporting a less-credible point estimate, even by an agent doing its honest best. And I would not loosen the parsimony tiebreaker. The tiebreaker fired once in thirteen iterations, on a 0.005% improvement, and was correct to fire. Both rules feel over-cautious when they do not fire and obviously necessary the moment they do.

Finally, a thresholding observation. The 0.5% CV-MSE keep tolerance turned out to be roughly the right value for this dataset and this number of iterations. A tighter threshold (0.1%) would have discarded iter06 (which improved CV-MSE by 12%, not by 0.1% — but only the first move past the keep ceiling matters here) and iter11 (correctly, as it turns out). A looser threshold (1.5%) would have kept iter07 (`momdad14` restriction) and iter08 (drop `married` on trimmed), neither of which the data really supports. The threshold is a hyperparameter; in a longer run, I would either justify it from theory (a bound on irreducible CV variance) or learn it from the data (compare CV folds against each other).

## 4.4 Broader Implications  *(with new literature paragraph)*

Autonomous research workflows will change academic research in ways that depend on which part of the research process is meant. They will not, in the near term, change the part where the human chooses the research question, defines the population of interest, or interprets the result in the context of prior literature. They will, increasingly, change the part where the human runs the experiments, fits the regressions, and assembles the robustness tables. That second part is the bulk of a researcher's hand-time, and it is where the search rules and the reward function determine the quality of the output. The lesson of this exercise is that good autonomous research is not produced by leaving an agent unsupervised; it is produced by designing the rules carefully enough that the agent's incentives align with the discipline's incentives.

This exercise sits inside an active research programme. Wang et al. (*Nature* 620, 2023) argue that scientific discovery is increasingly characterised by AI systems that "design, perform, and analyse experiments" with human review on outcomes rather than steps; the autoresearch paradigm is the engineering form of that claim. The reproducibility-crisis literature provides the urgency: Camerer et al. (2018) replicated only 13 of 21 *Science / Nature* social-science experiments, and Open Science Collaboration (2015) replicated 39 of 100 psychology results — failures that specification mining and undisclosed flexibility produced or amplified. The credibility-aware reward function in `program.md` is an attempt to encode the credibility-revolution response (Angrist & Pischke 2010) as machine-executable rules rather than as a referee's checklist. Two recent systems sit on the spectrum between this workflow and a full publication pipeline. Sakana AI's *AI-Scientist-v2* (2025) runs end-to-end discovery with first peer-reviewed AI-authored output; Schmidgall et al.'s *Agent Laboratory* (ICLR 2025) implements the three-phase Literature → Experiment → Report pipeline with reported 84% research-cost reduction. Both differ from this workflow on three dimensions: they target paper-level outputs rather than estimate-level outputs, they integrate literature search inside the loop rather than at checkpoints, and they accept higher dependence on a single fit-style metric. The Stanford REAP / CoPaper.AI *Awesome Agent Skills for Empirical Research* repository (the user's `research-repos-synthesis.md` §2.2 surveys it) codifies eight-stage robustness batteries (M1–M6 spec ladder, Wild cluster bootstrap, Oster δ*, Bacon decomposition) that this workflow's reward function approximates but does not run. The contribution of the present exercise, against that backdrop, is narrower and sharper: a reward function whose *defensive* rules (hard identification, parsimony tiebreaker, fixed multiverse) explicitly encode the credibility-revolution norms that make searches in this space publishable.

The most concrete risk is the one this workflow was built to defend against: overfitting to the metric and p-hacking by exhaustive search. The risk is structural rather than ethical. An agent given a single in-sample fit metric and a multi-day budget will eventually find a specification that nominally satisfies the metric and is uncomfortably close to noise. The defence is multi-dimensional rewards, hard rules at the credibility boundary, fixed multiverses for stability checks, and pre-registered keep/discard thresholds — the elements `program.md` formalises. None of these elements are conceptually new in econometrics, but writing them down as machine-executable rules is new, and the act of writing them down forces explicitness that traditional workflows rarely demand of themselves. There is a real possibility that autonomous workflows raise the methodological floor by making implicit norms explicit, even for researchers who never use one.

A second risk is the loss of scientific understanding: the agent finds what works faster than a human can extract a generalisable insight from why it worked. This is real and I felt it during the run. The biggest discovery in this exercise — that outlier trimming is the dominant fit lever for Card 1995, and that no control-set move on the full sample produces a comparable gain — would have been worth a paragraph of theoretical discussion if a human had reached it. In the autonomous run it appears in `results.tsv` as two KEEP rows and an inferred plateau pattern. The understanding is recoverable but only by reading the log carefully and reconstructing the narrative. This is the same problem as reading anyone else's careful empirical work, but it does change the unit of scholarly output. The paper-sized artifact that traditionally captures both result and reasoning becomes a smaller wrapper around a longer machine-generated journal.

Reproducibility, by contrast, becomes substantially easier. Every iteration in this run is a separate git commit with a one-line econometric justification, a `results.tsv` row with twenty-five typed fields, and a full diagnostic block in the run log. Replicating any specific row is one `git checkout` away. The `replication/` folder adds Stata (`.do`) and R/Quarto (`.qmd`) replication of the kept-best specification so reviewers in either ecosystem can verify the numbers without leaving their tool of choice. This is a real improvement over the implicit reproducibility of most empirical work, where the path from raw data to published table is reconstructed from memory and an under-commented Stata script. Autonomous workflows raise the floor here too: even a mediocre `program.md` makes the search history transparent in a way the typical empirical paper does not.

Attribution and authorship are more genuinely difficult. When the agent proposes a change, runs the experiment, and decides whether to keep it, the human's contribution is upstream — the question, the data, `prepare.py`, `program.md`, the three checkpoint criteria, and the final narrative. That contribution is substantial; it is also categorically different from the "I ran the regression and wrote the paper" contribution that current authorship norms reward. My tentative view is that authorship should follow the design of the research process, not the execution. The human who writes `program.md` is doing what the methods section of a paper used to do, only more rigorously, and that work should be credited. The agent should be acknowledged as an executor, the way computing resources are acknowledged, not as an author. The hard case is when the agent makes a meaningfully novel methodological move — for example, the trim-sweep at iter06 / iter09 / iter10, which I had not designed in. The right answer is probably that the agent gets methodological credit in the body of the paper but not in the byline. The disciplines will need to converge on a norm; I expect they will.

The right level of human oversight depends on three things: the objectivity of the metric, the structure of the search space, and the cost of a wrong answer. Karpathy's autoresearch has an objective metric (`val_bpb`), a closed-system search space (one Python file), and low cost per wrong answer (the next iteration corrects it). Full autonomy is appropriate there. This workflow has a composite metric that depends on a multiverse, a partly-narrative search space (identification stories), and moderate cost per wrong answer (a published return-to-schooling estimate is hard to retract). Human-in-the-loop oversight at three checkpoints is appropriate. A clinical trial or a policy evaluation would have a less objective metric, an open search space, and high cost per wrong answer; the appropriate oversight there is closer to continuous human approval of every meaningful decision. The general principle is that autonomy scales with the testability of the question and the reversibility of the conclusion. Returns-to-schooling on Card 1995 sits in the middle of that scale.

The single broader implication I take away is that the value of autonomous workflows is not in replacing the researcher but in making explicit what the researcher was already doing implicitly. The hours I spent writing `program.md` were not hours I would have spent running regressions; they were hours I would have spent making in-my-head decisions about whether to revert, whether to add a control, whether to trust a diagnostic. Writing those decisions down in machine-executable form is a form of methodological rigour that is independently valuable. That an agent can then execute against the rules is the proof that the rules were complete enough to execute against. The deeper change is the one in what the researcher writes down.
